# Life Flow

<!-- BLOCK 01: Born in Oizumi -->
So, where do I start? I was born in 1989, in a small town called Oizumi in Gunma Prefecture, about an hour and a half north of Tokyo.

<!-- BLOCK 02: Quiet kid into games and study -->
I was a pretty quiet kid — nothing flashy about me — and honestly, I spent most of my time on video games and studying.

<!-- BLOCK 03: School all in Gunma -->
I went through elementary, middle, and high school all in Gunma. Never moved, never transferred.

<!-- BLOCK 04: Go-home club but actually arcade -->
I didn't join any club at school either. I was what we call a "go-home club" kid, but instead of actually going home, I'd head straight to the arcade.

<!-- BLOCK 05: Fighting games -->
I was really into fighting games back then — Marvel vs. Capcom 2 and King of Fighters were my main ones. Pretty niche, I know.

<!-- BLOCK 06: Pneumothorax surgeries -->
One thing that shaped that whole period was my health, actually. I had this lung condition called pneumothorax, and between middle school and high school, I had surgery seven times — a mix of thoracoscopic and open-chest surgery. I was in and out of the hospital all the time.

<!-- BLOCK 07: All healed up -->
It's all healed up now, no follow-ups, totally fine, but yeah, it was a big part of growing up.

<!-- BLOCK 08: Waseda environmental engineering -->
For university, I went to Waseda University in Tokyo, and I studied resources and environmental engineering.

<!-- BLOCK 09: IT was first choice -->
To be honest, though, I actually wanted to go into IT since high school. My first choice was the industrial and management systems department, but I didn't make the cut, so I ended up in resources engineering instead.

<!-- BLOCK 10: Grad school rock failure testing -->
I stayed on for a master's degree and joined a lab focused on petroleum research. My main project was rock failure testing — basically, breaking rocks to measure how strong they are.

<!-- BLOCK 11: Original rig and simulations -->
I built my own original testing rig to get more accurate readings, and then fed those parameters into simulations.

<!-- BLOCK 12: Calibration method 100 to 10 hours -->
I also worked on this calibration method where I'd measure rock hardness every meter using a simpler, faster tool, and then convert those hardness values into proper strength values from compression tests. A single strength test takes over an hour per sample, but a hardness measurement only takes about a minute. So instead of spending 100 hours to get 100 data points, we could cover the same ground in about 10 hours. That was actually a really fun project.

<!-- BLOCK 13: Joined oil company in Akita -->
After my master's, I joined an oil and gas company — the same company I'm still with today. My first assignment was on-site in Akita, which is up in the north of Japan, just below Hokkaido.

<!-- BLOCK 14: Well inspections and three years there -->
I was doing well inspections — opening and closing valves to adjust oil production rates based on supply and demand, and a thing called sand production analysis, where you simulate how much you can produce before the rock around the well starts breaking down. I spent three years out there.

<!-- BLOCK 15: Chiba research center -->
Then I transferred to the technical research center in Chiba, and I spent five years there. I went deeper into sand production work — modeling actual real-world wells and running simulations to figure out the optimal production rate.

<!-- BLOCK 16: Career anxiety in Chiba -->
The work itself was solid, but somewhere in those Chiba years, I started getting kind of anxious about my future. Like, am I really going to be an oil engineer for the rest of my career?

<!-- BLOCK 17: IT itch and side project -->
And honestly, since high school I'd always wanted to be in IT, so that itch just kind of came back. I started a side project building social media automation tools, and that pulled me back into the IT world for real.

<!-- BLOCK 18: Internal transfer and AI today -->
Eventually, I made an internal transfer to the IT department, which is now called the Digital Strategy Division. These days I build automation tools to make business processes more efficient, and I'm leading the rollout of generative AI and AI agents across the company.

<!-- BLOCK 19: 11 years total breakdown -->
So all in all, I've been at this company for 11 years now — three years in Akita, five years in Chiba, and the rest in the Digital Strategy Division.

<!-- BLOCK 20: Philosophy 100 million yen goal -->
If I had to put my philosophy into one line: I'm going all in on earning money the smart way. The goal is to hit 100 million yen and build real wealth. That's where I'm pointed.
